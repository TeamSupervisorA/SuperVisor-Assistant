Analyzing Quantum Entanglement Dynamics in Superconducting Qubits

Elena Rostova, Department of Advanced Physics

Abstract

The pursuit of scalable quantum computing relies heavily on the ability to maintain and manipulate entangled states over extended periods. In this paper, we investigate the decoherence mechanisms affecting superconducting transmon qubits in a 2D lattice structure. Previous studies have primarily focused on isolated pairs, but our research expands this to multi-qubit interactions.

We propose a novel mitigation strategy utilizing continuous dynamic decoupling sequences. By applying meticulously timed microwave pulses, we observe a significant suppression of low-frequency noise. The empirical results demonstrate a 45% increase in the T2 coherence time, outperforming standard spin-echo techniques. This improvement is critical for executing deep quantum circuits required for complex algorithmic operations.

1. Methodology

Our experimental setup consists of a 9-qubit processor cooled to 15 mK in a dilution refrigerator. Control and readout are achieved via coplanar waveguide resonators. We implemented randomized benchmarking to assess the baseline fidelity of single and two-qubit gates.

The data was collected over a 72-hour continuous run, logging state tomography matrices at intervals of 5 minutes. The sheer volume of data necessitated the use of automated machine learning pipelines for noise classification.
